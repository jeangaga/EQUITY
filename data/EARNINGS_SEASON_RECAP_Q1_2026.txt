INDEX-LEVEL EARNINGS PICTURE

At index level, the season is materially stronger than expected. EPS and revenue surprise breadth are unusually high — Q1 S&P 500 blended EPS growth is tracking around 27% with surprise rates above 80% on both EPS and revenue, revenue growth is broad at roughly 11%, and Q1 net margin is the highest in over 15 years. This is not merely "resilience"; it is a major positive revision season at the index level. The caveat is that headline EPS growth is flattered by several very large one-off or non-operating items concentrated in mega-cap tech and one-off items at Ford and GE Vernova, so the operating-quality picture is somewhat less spectacular than reported EPS suggests. Forward margins are also under more scrutiny than current margins, with consensus margin estimates being trimmed in most sectors as commodity, tariff and fuel costs flow through. Q2 guidance is mixed rather than uniformly positive, with negative pre-announcements modestly outnumbering positive ones.

US GROWTH

The earnings season argues against an imminent US hard landing, but it has revealed clearer late-cycle stresses than the early bank cluster suggested. Capital markets reopened, capital-markets banks executed broadly, aerospace / defense / power equipment names confirmed multi-year backlog visibility. Texas Instruments and Nucor printed real cyclical reversals, while Boeing showed turnaround credibility for the first time. The growth signal remains barbelled: AI infrastructure, defense, power, capital markets and selected industrial recoveries are strong; fuel-sensitive transport, rate-sensitive lenders, mass-consumer staples and middle-income credit pockets remain more uneven.

US CONSUMER

The US consumer is intact but K-shaped. American Express, Visa, Starbucks (post-turnaround), L'Oréal and Philip Morris show premium and brand-led demand remains healthy. PepsiCo, Procter & Gamble, Coca-Cola and General Motors show the mass consumer is responsive when affordability and innovation are deployed but cannot absorb additional cost shocks easily. Ally pushed back against the auto-credit stress narrative; Progressive confirmed P&C underwriting discipline; American Airlines confirmed travel demand is strong but earnings are being absorbed by fuel. Tesla flagged inventory build, the clearest near-term EV demand caution. UPS deliberately shed volume to improve mix. Payments data and premium travel/entertainment still push back against recession risk, but higher fuel and energy prices are now the forward risk to lower-income discretionary spending.

FINANCIAL CONDITIONS / CREDIT

Financial conditions are not easy, but they are open enough to sustain activity. JPM, BAC, MS, GS and Citi confirmed trading and capital-markets reopening, while credit remained broadly benign outside specific noise. The bank cohort is sharply differentiated on NII: BAC raised the FY guide, Citi reaffirmed NII ex-Markets, JPM trimmed the total NII guide, WFC printed a sequential NII decline and a revenue/interest-income miss, and TFC cut the FY guide. Custody banks (BK, STT) confirmed a beat cycle; FITB confirmed early Comerica integration accretion; Schwab confirmed cash sorting easing. Insurance and consumer finance (PGR, ALLY) confirmed cycle stabilization. The capital-markets reopening is broad rather than uniformly clean — GS FICC weakness and WFC NII compression are the offsetting datapoints. The system signal is not credit accident; it is a transition from NII-led earnings to fees, markets and selective lending, with deposit / rate sensitivity now the binding line between winners and losers.

AI / CAPEX / INFRASTRUCTURE

AI is the dominant incremental earnings impulse and estimate-revision engine, but the season's strength also reflects broad revenue surprise breadth, margins, financials, materials and energy. TSM, ASMI, LRCX, TXN, INTC, QCOM, CDNS, CLS, GEV, VRT, NEE, AMT, GOOGL, MSFT, META and AMZN all point to a broadening cycle across foundry, semicap, EDA, analog power, custom silicon, EMS, data-center power and cooling, gas turbines, grid, utilities and hyperscaler cloud. The April 28-29 mega-cap cluster confirmed AI demand is still accelerating: Azure +40%, Google Cloud +63%, AWS +28%, Cloud backlog at $460B+ for Alphabet, $627B commercial RPO for Microsoft. The new tension is capital allocation: hyperscaler 2026 capex now sits at $180B-$190B (Alphabet), ~$190B (Microsoft), $125B-$145B (Meta, raised), and Tesla raised its own to $25B+. The external debate is no longer whether AI capex is real; it is whether each dollar of capex is converting into cloud revenue, backlog monetization, margin stability and FCF before investor patience resets. Importantly, part of the capex escalation reflects memory and component inflation rather than pure incremental capacity, which matters for FCF interpretation across semis and hyperscalers.

EUROPE

Europe is better than feared, but the improvement is selective and partly energy/resources-skewed rather than a broad reflation signal. Non-energy revenue forecasts remain weak even as non-energy earnings stabilize, and aggregate European earnings growth still trails the S&P 500 by a wide margin. Within that frame: ASMI confirms European semicap is tightly plugged into the AI cycle. L'Oréal delivered the largest single-day gain since 2008 and pushed back against the slowing-beauty narrative through China selective recovery. Eni and BP showed integrated-oil resilience under the energy shock, with Eni raising CFFO guidance 20% and BP monetizing refining / trading despite buyback suspension. Novartis showed that pharma transition is painful but priority brands are growing. Spotify confirmed European-listed software / digital quality compounding.

ASIA

Asia remains the hard manufacturing core of the AI and industrial cycle. TSM is the cleanest expression of supply-constrained AI demand, with FY USD growth raised to >30% and 66% gross margin. Rio Tinto and BHP indicate China industrial demand is uneven but not collapsing, with Simandou first shipment and BHP copper guidance moving to upper-half. Vale missed on BRL-driven cost inflation but base metals diversification improved. The Asian signal is stronger in industrial / AI supply chains than in consumer demand.

CROSS-ASSET PM TAKE

The strongest earnings impulses are AI infrastructure capex demand, capital markets reopening, defense / power equipment, and selective cyclical industry. The main constraints are Middle East fuel / logistics shocks, NII compression in spread-sensitive banks, mega-cap reported-EPS distortion and capex ROI discrimination, software margin scrutiny, and consumer cost pressure. Setup risk is high and beat rewards are unusually small, especially where guidance, capex or margin conversion fail to clear elevated expectations — NOW, NFLX, TSLA, GE, VRT, CLS, RMBS and SCHW all sold off on operationally good prints. But indices remain at or near highs, earnings revisions are positive, and the market is still funding AI winners where revenue conversion is visible (Alphabet, semis, cloud beneficiaries). The cleanest framing is high dispersion, not broad fragility.

BOTTOM LINE

Earnings are strong, but increasingly concentrated, capex-intensive, quality-adjusted and setup-sensitive. The season is materially better than expected at the index level, with broad revenue surprises, historically high current margins, and AI as the dominant incremental impulse. The qualifications are: reported EPS at the index level is flattered by mega-cap one-offs ($37.7B at Alphabet, $16.8B at Amazon, $8.03B at Meta, $2.8B at Netflix, $1.3B tariff benefit at Ford, $4.5B M&A gain at GEV); AI capex is no longer rewarded unconditionally and is being sorted by revenue conversion; bank NII is sharply bifurcated; Middle East / fuel shocks have moved from macro headline to P&L variable; and forward margin assumptions are being trimmed even as current margins are at record highs. Operating income, FCF and adjusted measures are safer than reported EPS for cross-cohort comparison.

ENERGY EPS-QUALITY CAVEAT
Energy reported EPS in this season requires adjustment for war-driven timing effects, hedging / accounting mismatches and trading / refining windfalls. Cash flow and forward price assumptions are more informative than reported net income across the sector. Outside the covered list, Exxon reported $4.2B earnings or $8.8B excluding unfavorable timing effects and an identified item, and Chevron flagged hedging / timing effects expected to cut earnings and operating cash flow with partial reversal later. BP and Eni windfalls and SLB Middle East disruption fit the same broader frame.
